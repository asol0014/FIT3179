Air Travel in Australia through History

Using Australian flight data, let's take a journey through time to investigate trends and the impact of hisorical events on Australia's aviation industry. Data dating as far back as 1985 reveals how world events have shaped air travel and how the industry has grown with an ever more connected world.

Incoming International Passengers

This world map depicts where short term visitors have flown in from. From the distribution of lines, we see that Australia is well connected with Europe and Asia. Likewise, The United States is consistently in the top ten countries visitors come from. Conversely, Australia is less well connected with South American countries, with flights to 4 countries on the continent. The lack of flights to Africa is illustrated too, as currently the only direct route is from Johannesburg to Perth. Other African countries passengers could have travelled to via connecting flights were not in the dataset.

Which are Australia's busiest airports?

Pictured below are commercial airports in Australia, and the number of domestic or international flights that have landed or taken off there since 1985. Sydney Airport is Australia's busiest airport, with Melbourne and Brisbane following. This is unsurprising, as Sydney opened its international airport in the late 1940s, whilst Melbourne opened its doors to international travellers in 1970 when its international airport was unveiled.

These maps also illustrate how Australia's airports are concentrated along the East Coast, which aligns with how approximately 80% of Australians live on the East Coast of the country. Note that not all airports have domestic and international terminals, and some international flights service few international routes. For example, Hobart's only regular international flight is between Aukland New Zealand. Simiarly, whilst Townsville has international airport status, it has no regular scheduled passenger flights currently.

How has the popularity of air travel changed in Australia?

With Sydney Airport clearly the busiest airport in the country, with just shy of 10 million flights in and out since its opening, catching a flight to travel the country or abroad is clearly a popular undertaking. But air travel has not always been this popular or abundant. To service these passengers, the number of flights departing Australian airports over time has almost doubled since 1985.

That said, flashpoints in history have moulded this number, with the number of flights departing and arriving in the country slowing at 3 distinct points since 1985. Firstly, you'll notice the COVID-19 pandemic caused the most significant drop in March 2020, due to restrictions and risks with international travel for the threat to health it caused. The 9/11 attacks in America also marked a reduction in flights, again with threats to safety in air travel and worry caused by the attacks. In both instances, their impacts are felt years after with pre-COVID flight numbers only just returning, and the post 9/11 flight numbers recovering almost 3 years later.

Switch between different airports to see how flights decreased more or less, and how long they took to recover from these historical events.

Note also the decline in August and September 1989, which was caused by a pilots' strike in Australia. The pilots argued that if the government treated them the same way it treated other employee groups, they would only work the standard hours of those groups, 9 to 5. A few days before the strikes began on 18 August 1989, former Prime Minister Bob Hawke allowed Royal Australian Air Force and international flights to operate in order to ease disruptions, rather than negotiating agreements with the pilots. Ultimately, agreements were never negotiated and the dispute was resolved after many pilots resigned to avoid potential litigation from their employers. The strikes had a significant impact on the tourism sector, particularly domestic tourism, highlighting the strong interconnection and dependence between the aviation and tourism industries.

Events like the COVID-19 pandemic, 9/11 attacks, and pilot strikes have shaped the number of flights, but do passenger trends reflect these trends too?

International Passenger Arrivals & Departures

Only some events that impacted flight numbers show a decline in the number of passengers travelling. Most obviously, the COVID-19 pandemic reduced the number of international travellers significantly, as people were in lockdown. Zooming into the time after the 9/11 attacks, passenger numbers continued on their same trend, suggesting whilst there were less flights, those flights were more full. Potentially, it was the lack of flights to and from other countries causing the decline in 2001 illustrated earlier. The lack of a drop in 1989 reflects how it was largely domestic tourism in Australia that was impacted by the Australian pilots strike.
